# Letters I Never Sent

## A Novel

### By Walusimbi Leon (SGSS)

---

## Part One: New York

### Chapter 1: The Box in the Basement

The bookshop was called The Strand of Memory, and it sat on a narrow street in Greenwich Village like a secret that had forgotten to keep itself hidden.

Naomi Chen found it on a Tuesday in October, during one of those perfect New York autumn days when the light turns golden and the city smells of roasted chestnuts and possibility. She had been walking without destination, a rare luxury for a magazine editor whose days were measured in deadlines and word counts, whose nights were measured in glasses of wine and the particular exhaustion of someone who spent other people's words for a living.

The shop's facade was unremarkable — a faded green awning, a window display of books stacked in precarious towers, a hand-painted sign that read: "The Strand of Memory — We Buy, We Sell, We Remember." She almost walked past. Almost. But something — the name, maybe, or the way the light fell through the window, or the fact that she had nowhere else to be — made her stop.

Inside, the shop was a labyrinth. Shelves rose to the ceiling, connected by rolling ladders and catwalks that turned the space into a three-dimensional puzzle of paper and ink. The air smelled of old leather and vanilla, the particular scent of books that have been loved and abandoned and loved again.

Naomi moved through the aisles with the practiced eye of someone who had spent her life among words. She was thirty-two, Japanese-American, born in San Francisco, raised between two cultures that had taught her to see beauty in different things — her mother's wabi-sabi, her father's pragmatism, the particular grace of being neither fully one thing nor the other.

She was looking for nothing in particular. She found everything.

In the basement level — a cramped, low-ceilinged space that smelled of damp and dust — she found a box.

It was sitting on a shelf between a water-stained copy of *Love in the Time of Cholera* and a stack of National Geographic magazines from the 1970s. The box was small, wooden, dark with age. A simple brass latch held it closed. There was a piece of tape on the lid with a name written in faded ink: **Kenji Tanaka — Personal Correspondence.**

She opened it.

Inside were letters. Dozens of them. Tied with a silk ribbon the color of old roses. The paper was cream-colored, thick, the kind of paper that had been chosen with care. The handwriting was precise, elegant — the strokes of someone who had learned calligraphy and never forgotten it.

She unfolded the first one.

---

*Dear Maren,*

*I am writing this letter on the plane between New York and Paris. The window is dark — we are somewhere over the Atlantic, where the ocean and the sky become the same thing. I have been thinking about you since we said goodbye at the airport. I have been thinking about the way you laughed when I told you I was afraid of flying. You said: "Kenji, you are a photographer. You are supposed to chase the sky, not fear it." And I said: "I chase the sky from the ground. There is a difference." And you said: "Not if you learn to let go."*

*I have been trying to let go, Maren. Not of you — never of you. But of the fear that this — whatever this is between us — will end before it begins.*

*I love you. I have loved you since the day you walked into my gallery in SoHo and stood in front of my photograph of the Brooklyn Bridge for twenty minutes without moving. When you finally turned to me, your eyes were wet, and you said: "You see the city the way I hear it." And I knew. I knew that you were the person I had been waiting for my entire life.*

*But I am a coward. I am writing this letter, and I will not send it. I will add it to the collection — the letters I write to you but never have the courage to deliver. The letters that live in a box in my apartment, accumulating like dust, like regret, like all the words I should have said when you were standing right in front of me.*

*Maybe one day I will be brave enough. Maybe one day I will knock on your door and say the things I have written. But today is not that day.*

*Today I am on a plane, and the ocean is below me, and you are in New York, and I am going to Paris, and the distance between us is measured not in miles but in the courage I do not have.*

*I love you. I love you. I love you.*

*Kenji*

---

Naomi read the letter twice. Then she unfolded the next one. And the next. And the next.

There were forty-seven letters. All addressed to Maren. None of them sent. They spanned three years — from 2015 to 2018 — and they told a story that unfolded like a novel: a love that began in a gallery, grew through shared silences and midnight conversations, and was eventually consumed by the particular cowardice of two people who loved each other too much to risk the words.

The last letter was different. It was shorter. The handwriting was shaky, as though the writer's hand had trembled.

---

*Dear Maren,*

*I am in the hospital. The doctors say it is serious. I do not know how much time I have, but I know that I have wasted too much of it.*

*I should have sent these letters. I should have knocked on your door. I should have told you the truth when you were standing right in front of me, your eyes wet, your hands trembling, your heart open like a book I was too afraid to read.*

*But I did not. And now it is too late.*

*I am leaving these letters in the care of Mr. Alvarez at the bookshop on Bleecker Street. He will keep them safe. And if you ever find them — if fate, which has always been cruel to us, decides to be kind for once — know this:*

*Every word was true. Every silence was a lie. And I loved you. I loved you from the first moment. I loved you until the last.*

*Kenji*

---

Naomi closed the box. Her hands were shaking.

She sat in the basement of the bookshop for a long time, the box in her lap, the letters resting against her chest like a heart that had stopped beating but had not stopped loving.

She did not know Kenji Tanaka. She did not know Maren. But she knew this: these letters deserved to be read. They deserved to reach the person they were written for. They deserved to be more than dust and silence.

She was a journalist. She told stories for a living. And this — this was a story that needed to be told.

She found the shopkeeper, a thin, dignified man with silver hair and kind eyes, and asked about the box.

"Ah," he said, when she showed it to him. "The Tanaka letters. Yes. He brought them here three years ago. Just before he died. He asked me to keep them safe. He said someone would come looking for them eventually."

"Did he say who?"

"He said the universe would send the right person. He was a romantic." The shopkeeper smiled. "Clearly, the universe sent you."

### Chapter 2: The Trail of Ink

Naomi began her investigation the way she began every story — with research.

Kenji Tanaka had been a photographer. Born in Kyoto, raised in San Francisco, educated at RISD. He had moved to New York in his twenties, had built a career photographing cities — the particular beauty of urban landscapes at dawn, the way light fell through the windows of old buildings, the faces of strangers caught in moments of private joy or sorrow. His work had been exhibited in galleries in New York, Paris, Tokyo, and London. He had been celebrated, collected, reviewed in every major art publication.

And then, in 2018, he had died. Pancreatic cancer. Forty-one years old.

Naomi found his obituary in the *New York Times*. It was brief, respectful, devoid of personal detail. It mentioned his work, his exhibitions, his surviving sister in Kyoto. It did not mention Maren.

She searched for Maren.

The letters gave her clues — a gallery in SoHo where they had met, a restaurant in the West Village where they had their first dinner, a park in Brooklyn where they had walked together on autumn evenings. Naomi visited each place, tracing the geography of a love affair she had never witnessed but now knew by heart.

The gallery was gone — replaced by a luxury condominium. The restaurant was still there, but the owner was new, the menu different, the particular booth where Kenji and Maren had sat now occupied by a couple sharing pasta and wine.

But the park was still there. Prospect Park, in Brooklyn. The bench where Kenji had written his first letter was still there, weathered and gray, facing a pond where ducks paddled in lazy circles.

Naomi sat on the bench and read the first letter again. And she understood — with the particular clarity that comes from sitting in the exact place where something happened — that this was not just a love story. It was a story about the cost of silence. About the words we leave unspoken and the lives they would have changed if we had been brave enough to say them.

She needed to find Maren.

---

The trail led to Paris.

One of the letters mentioned a concert — "the Debussy recital at the Théâtre des Champs-Élysées, where you closed your eyes and I watched the music move through you like wind through water." Naomi found the concert program online. The pianist was listed. She found the pianist's social media. And through the pianist's social media, she found a photograph — a woman with auburn hair and freckles, standing in front of the Eiffel Tower, laughing at something outside the frame.

The caption read: *Maren Ellis, 2019.*

Maren was alive. And she was in Paris.

Naomi booked a flight.

---

### Chapter 3: Paris, Again

She found Maren in a café on the Left Bank, sitting by the window with a cup of coffee and a book. She was older than Naomi had expected — mid-forties, with the kind of face that had been beautiful in youth and had become something better in middle age: lined, lived-in, expressive. Her auburn hair was streaked with gray. Her eyes were the color of whiskey, and they held a particular quality of attention that Naomi recognized from Kenji's letters — the way he had described Maren looking at things, as though she were seeing not just the surface but the depth beneath.

Naomi approached the table. "Maren Ellis?"

The woman looked up. "Yes?"

"My name is Naomi Chen. I'm a journalist from New York. I found something — someone — that I think you should know about."

She sat down. She placed the box on the table between them. And she told the story of how she had found it, in the basement of a bookshop in Greenwich Village, on a Tuesday in October.

Maren stared at the box. Her face did not change. But her hands — Naomi watched her hands — began to tremble.

"Kenji," she whispered.

"You knew him."

"Knew him." Maren laughed, a sound that was half joy and half grief. "I loved him. I loved him for three years. And he never told me."

"He wrote you forty-seven letters."

"Forty-seven." Maren's eyes filled with tears. "That sounds like him. He could write anything. He could write the alphabet and make it sound like a love poem. But he couldn't say it. He couldn't say it to my face."

She opened the box. She unfolded the first letter. She read it, her lips moving silently, her tears falling onto the cream-colored paper.

When she finished, she looked up at Naomi. "He died thinking I didn't know."

"He died thinking he never told you."

"But I knew. I always knew. I could see it in the way he looked at me, in the way his hands trembled when I touched him, in the way he would go quiet in the middle of a conversation, as though he were trying to find the words and couldn't. I knew he loved me. I just — I was waiting for him to say it."

"Why?"

"Because I was afraid too." Maren wiped her eyes. "We were both cowards. We were both standing on the edge of something beautiful, and neither of us had the courage to jump."

Naomi sat in silence for a long time. The café buzzed around them — the clink of cups, the murmur of conversations, the sound of Paris being Paris. And in the silence between Naomi and Maren, something shifted — a recognition, a kinship, the particular bond that forms between two people who have witnessed the same story from different angles.

"He would have wanted you to have these," Naomi said.

"He would have wanted me to have him." Maren folded the letter carefully, placed it back in the box, and closed the lid. "Thank you. For finding them. For bringing them to me. For finishing the story he couldn't."

"I didn't finish it. You did. By being here. By being alive. By being the person he wrote to."

Maren smiled — a smile that held forty-seven letters and three years of silence and the particular beauty of a love that had been felt but never spoken.

"Would you like to stay for coffee?" she asked. "I think I have more story to tell."

"I would love that."

## Part Two: The Letters

### Chapter 4: What the Letters Say

They spent three days together. Three days of coffee and walks and long conversations in Maren's apartment overlooking the Seine, the letters spread across the kitchen table like a map of a country that no longer existed.

Maren told Naomi the story that the letters only hinted at. How she had met Kenji at his gallery opening in SoHo — how she had stood in front of his photograph of the Brooklyn Bridge for twenty minutes, not because she was fascinated by the bridge but because she was fascinated by the way he had seen it. How he had approached her, shy and trembling, and said: "You're the only person who has ever looked at that photograph the way I meant it to be seen."

How they had walked through the city together — through the streets of SoHo and the parks of Brooklyn, through the markets of Chelsea and the galleries of the Lower East Side. How they had developed their own language, their own rituals, their own particular way of being together that was unlike anything either of them had known before.

"He would bring me photographs," Maren said. "Not prints — the negatives. He would hold them up to the light and say: 'This is how I see you. Not the finished version. The raw one. The one with all the light and shadow still in it.' And I would look at the negatives, and I would see what he meant. I would see myself the way he saw me — not as a finished portrait but as a work in progress, full of potential, full of beauty that hadn't yet been developed."

"Why didn't he send the letters?"

Maren was quiet for a long time. "Because he was afraid. Not of rejection — he could handle rejection. He was afraid of the opposite. He was afraid that if he said the words, they would become real, and if they became real, they could be lost. And he had lost so much already — his mother when he was twelve, his father when he was twenty, his country when he left Japan. He had built a life around the principle of not holding on too tightly, because everything he had ever held on to had eventually slipped away."

"So he held on by not holding on."

"Exactly. And I let him. Because I understood. Because I was afraid too."

Naomi looked at the letters — forty-seven of them, each one a small masterpiece of longing and restraint. She thought about her own life, her own silences, the things she had left unsaid to the people she had loved.

"What would you say to him now?" she asked.

Maren picked up the last letter — the one Kenji had written from the hospital, the one with the shaky handwriting. She held it against her chest.

"I would say: I knew. I always knew. And I loved you too. Not despite the silence — because of it. Because the silence was where we lived. It was the house we built together, the room where we were most ourselves. And I would not trade it. I would not trade a single moment of the silence we shared."

She paused. "But I would also say: send the letters next time. Life is too short for unread love letters."

---

### Chapter 5: The Story She Couldn't Write

Naomi returned to New York with the story in her notebook and a weight in her chest that she could not name.

She wrote the article. She wrote it beautifully, the way she wrote everything — with precision and feeling, with the careful attention to detail that had made her one of the most respected editors at *The New Yorker*. She wrote about Kenji and Maren, about the letters, about the bookshop, about the way love survives in the spaces between words.

But when she finished, she knew it was wrong.

It was wrong because it was incomplete. The letters told a story, but they did not tell the whole story. The whole story was the silence — the three years of unspoken words, the missed chances, the moments when Kenji could have knocked on Maren's door and chose not to. That was the real story. Not the letters themselves, but the silence that had made them necessary.

She deleted the article. She started again.

And this time, she wrote about silence. About the architecture of absence. About the way we build walls out of the things we don't say. About the way love lives not in words but in the spaces between words — in the glances, the touches, the moments when two people look at each other and know everything without speaking.

She wrote about Kenji and Maren. But she also wrote about herself. About the phone call she had never made to her mother in San Francisco. About the letter she had never written to her ex-boyfriend, the one who had loved her in a language she hadn't learned to speak. About the silence she carried in her chest like a stone, heavy and smooth and impossible to put down.

The article was published. It won a National Magazine Award. But more than that — it changed things. People wrote to her, hundreds of them, telling their own stories of unsent letters, of words left unspoken, of love that lived in silence.

And one day, she received a letter of her own.

---

It arrived on a Wednesday, in a cream-colored envelope, with no return address. The handwriting was precise, elegant — the strokes of someone who had learned calligraphy and never forgotten it.

She opened it.

---

*Dear Naomi,*

*I am writing this letter because I read your article. I read it on a Tuesday, in a café in Tokyo, and I cried. I cried because you told a story that I recognized. I cried because you wrote about silence the way I have always felt it — as a language, not an absence. As a home, not a void.*

*I am a photographer. I live in Tokyo. I have spent my life photographing the spaces between things — the gap between buildings, the pause between heartbeats, the silence between words. I thought I was the only one who saw the world this way. I thought I was alone.*

*But your article told me I was not. It told me that there are others — others who love in silence, who speak in gestures, who find meaning in the spaces where words should be but aren't.*

*I would like to meet you. Not because I am lonely, but because I am curious. I would like to sit across from you in a café and share a silence — the kind of silence that says everything words cannot.*

*If you are willing, I will be at the Café de Flore in Paris on the first Saturday of November. I will be the one sitting by the window, with a camera in my lap and a heart full of words I have never sent.*

*Yours, in silence,*
*Hiroshi Sato*

---

Naomi read the letter three times. Then she folded it carefully, placed it in the box of Kenji's letters — the box Maren had given her, the box she now kept on her desk as a reminder of the cost of silence — and booked a flight to Paris.

## Part Three: The Silence

### Chapter 6: Café de Flore

She found him on the first Saturday of November, sitting by the window of Café de Flore, a camera in his lap, his eyes on the street. He was Japanese, mid-thirties, with the quiet intensity of someone who spent his life observing. He looked up when she approached, and she saw his eyes — dark, warm, curious — and she knew.

She knew the way she had known about Kenji's letters. The way Maren had known about Kenji's love. The way every person who has ever loved in silence knows — not with the ears, not with the mind, but with the deep, wordless certainty of the heart.

"Naomi?" he said.

"Hiroshi."

He stood. He extended his hand. Then he withdrew it, smiled, and signed: *In Japan, we bow to people we have already shared silence with.*

She smiled back. *In America, we shake hands.*

*Then let us do neither. Let us sit. And let us share the silence.*

They sat. The café hummed around them — the clink of cups, the murmur of French, the particular sound of Paris in autumn, when the city turns golden and the air smells of rain and possibility.

They sat in silence for a long time. Not an awkward silence, not an empty silence, but the kind of silence that is full — full of presence, full of attention, full of the particular intimacy that comes from two people who are willing to be still together.

And in that silence, Naomi heard everything she needed to hear.

She heard the rhythm of his breathing. She heard the way his hands moved when he was thinking — small, precise gestures, the hands of a photographer, a man who spent his life framing the world. She heard the way his eyes softened when he looked at her, the way his mouth curved slightly at the corners, the way his whole body leaned toward her, as though she were gravity and he were a planet caught in her orbit.

She heard the letters he had never written. The words he had never said. The love he had never spoken.

And she understood — with the same clarity she had felt in the basement of the bookshop, with the same certainty she had felt reading Kenji's letters — that this was how love begins. Not with words. Not with declarations. Not with the grand gestures that novels and films promise. But with silence. With presence. With the willingness to sit across from another person and say, without speaking: *I see you. I am here. I am not going anywhere.*

"Hiroshi," she said.

"Yes?"

"I think I would like to write you a letter."

He smiled. "I would like to receive it."

"But I won't send it."

"Then I will come find it."

She laughed. He laughed. And the silence between them — the beautiful, full, living silence — held them both, like a cupped hand holding water, like a box holding letters, like a heart holding love.

---

They married in Kyoto, in the garden of Hiroshi's grandmother's house, under a cherry tree that had been planted the year Kenji Tanaka was born. Maren came from Paris. Naomi's mother came from San Francisco. They didn't all speak the same language, but they shared tears, and laughter, and the particular joy of watching two people who had learned to love in silence finally find someone who understood.

Hiroshi took photographs of the wedding — not the ceremony, not the guests, not the obvious moments, but the spaces between: the pause before the kiss, the silence after the vows, the moment when Naomi's mother and Hiroshi's grandmother held hands across the language barrier and wept.

He titled the collection "Letters I Never Sent." And when Naomi saw them, she understood.

They were love letters. Every one. Not written in words, but in light. Not sent by mail, but by lens. Not read by eyes, but by hearts.

And in the silence between the photographs — in the spaces where the words should have been but weren't — the love lived. Quietly. Patiently. Eternally.

## About This Book

**Letters I Never Sent** is a love story about the words we leave unspoken, the love letters we never mail, and the second chances that arrive when we least expect them. Set across New York, Paris, and Tokyo, it is a story about a journalist who finds a box of unsent love letters and discovers that the most important stories are the ones we never tell.

It is a story for anyone who has ever loved someone in silence — and for anyone who has ever believed that the most beautiful words are the ones that live in the spaces between speaking.
