Letters I Never Sent

A Novel

By Walusimbi Leon (SGSS)

✨ Version 2.0 — International Edition

About This Book

Letters I Never Sent is a love story about the words we leave unspoken, the love letters we never mail, and the second chances that arrive when we least expect them. Set across New York, Paris, and Tokyo, it is a story about a journalist who finds a box of unsent love letters and discovers that the most important stories are the ones we never tell.

Part One: New York

Chapter 1: The Box in the Basement

New York City

The bookshop was called The Strand of Memory, and it sat on a narrow street in Greenwich Village like a secret that had forgotten to keep itself hidden.

Naomi Chen found it on a Tuesday in October, during one of those perfect New York autumn days when the light turns golden and the city smells of roasted chestnuts and possibility. She had been walking without destination, a rare luxury for a magazine editor whose days were measured in deadlines and word counts.

Inside, the shop was a labyrinth. Shelves rose to the ceiling, connected by rolling ladders and catwalks. The air smelled of old leather and vanilla.

In the basement level — a cramped, low-ceilinged space that smelled of damp and dust — she found a box.

It was sitting on a shelf between a water-stained copy of Love in the Time of Cholera and a stack of National Geographic magazines from the 1970s. The box was small, wooden, dark with age. A simple brass latch held it closed. There was a piece of tape on the lid with a name written in faded ink: Kenji Tanaka — Personal Correspondence.

She opened it. Inside were letters. Dozens of them. Tied with a silk ribbon the color of old roses.

She unfolded the first one.

Dear Maren,

I am writing this letter on the plane between New York and Paris. I have been thinking about you since we said goodbye at the airport. You said: "Kenji, you are a photographer. You are supposed to chase the sky, not fear it."

I love you. I have loved you since the day you walked into my gallery in SoHo and stood in front of my photograph of the Brooklyn Bridge for twenty minutes without moving.

But I am a coward. I am writing this letter, and I will not send it. I will add it to the collection — the letters I write to you but never have the courage to deliver.

I love you. I love you. I love you.

Kenji

Naomi read the letter twice. Then she unfolded the next one. And the next. And the next.

There were forty-seven letters. All addressed to Maren. None of them sent. They spanned three years and told a story that unfolded like a novel: a love that began in a gallery, grew through shared silences and midnight conversations, and was consumed by the cowardice of two people who loved each other too much to risk the words.

The last letter was different. The handwriting was shaky.

Dear Maren,

I am in the hospital. I should have sent these letters. I should have knocked on your door. I should have told you the truth when you were standing right in front of me.

Every word was true. Every silence was a lie. And I loved you. I loved you from the first moment. I loved you until the last.

Kenji

Naomi closed the box. Her hands were shaking. She did not know Kenji Tanaka. She did not know Maren. But she knew this: these letters deserved to be read. They deserved to reach the person they were written for.

Chapter 2: The Trail of Ink

Naomi began her investigation the way she began every story — with research. Kenji Tanaka had been a photographer. Born in Kyoto, raised in San Francisco, educated at RISD. He had moved to New York in his twenties, built a career photographing cities. And then, in 2018, he had died. Pancreatic cancer. Forty-one years old.

The letters gave her clues — a gallery in SoHo, a restaurant in the West Village, a park in Brooklyn. Naomi visited each place, tracing the geography of a love affair she had never witnessed but now knew by heart.

The trail led to Paris. One of the letters mentioned a concert — "the Debussy recital at the Théâtre des Champs-Élysées." Naomi found the concert program online. Through the pianist's social media, she found a photograph — a woman with auburn hair and freckles, standing in front of the Eiffel Tower.

The caption read: Maren Ellis, 2019.

Maren was alive. And she was in Paris.

Chapter 3: Paris, Again

She found Maren in a café on the Left Bank, sitting by the window with a cup of coffee and a book. Naomi placed the box on the table between them and told the story of how she had found it.

Maren stared at the box. "Kenji," she whispered.

"He wrote you forty-seven letters."

"Forty-seven." Maren's eyes filled with tears. "That sounds like him. He could write anything. But he couldn't say it to my face."

"He died thinking he never told you."

"But I knew. I always knew. I could see it in the way he looked at me. I was waiting for him to say it."

"Why?"

"Because I was afraid too." Maren wiped her eyes. "We were both cowards. We were both standing on the edge of something beautiful, and neither of us had the courage to jump."

They spent three days together. Three days of coffee and walks and long conversations, the letters spread across the kitchen table like a map of a country that no longer existed.

Maren told Naomi the story the letters only hinted at. How Kenji would bring her negatives — not prints — and hold them up to the light. "This is how I see you," he would say. "Not the finished version. The raw one."

"What would you say to him now?" Naomi asked.

Maren picked up the last letter. "I would say: I knew. I always knew. And I loved you too. Not despite the silence — because of it." She paused. "But I would also say: send the letters next time. Life is too short for unread love letters."

Part Two: The Letters

Chapter 4: What the Letters Say

Naomi returned to New York with the story in her notebook and a weight in her chest that she could not name. She wrote the article — beautifully, precisely, the way she wrote everything. But when she finished, she knew it was wrong.

It was wrong because it was incomplete. The letters told a story, but the real story was the silence — the three years of unspoken words, the missed chances, the moments when Kenji could have knocked on Maren's door and chose not to.

She deleted the article. She started again. And this time, she wrote about silence. About the architecture of absence. About the way love lives not in words but in the spaces between words.

The article won a National Magazine Award. But more than that — people wrote to her, hundreds of them, telling their own stories of unsent letters.

And one day, she received a letter of her own.

Dear Naomi,

I am writing this letter because I read your article. I read it on a Tuesday, in a café in Tokyo, and I cried.

I am a photographer. I live in Tokyo. I have spent my life photographing the spaces between things — the gap between buildings, the pause between heartbeats, the silence between words.

I would like to meet you. Not because I am lonely, but because I am curious. I would like to sit across from you in a café and share a silence.

If you are willing, I will be at the Café de Flore in Paris on the first Saturday of November.

Yours, in silence,

Hiroshi Sato

Part Three: The Silence

Chapter 5: Café de Flore

She found him on the first Saturday of November, sitting by the window of Café de Flore, a camera in his lap, his eyes on the street. He was Japanese, mid-thirties, with the quiet intensity of someone who spent his life observing.

He stood. He extended his hand. Then he withdrew it, smiled, and signed: In Japan, we bow to people we have already shared silence with.

She smiled back. In America, we shake hands.

Then let us do neither. Let us sit. And let us share the silence.

They sat. The café hummed around them — the clink of cups, the murmur of French, the particular sound of Paris in autumn.

They sat in silence for a long time. Not an awkward silence, not an empty silence, but the kind of silence that is full — full of presence, full of attention, full of the particular intimacy that comes from two people who are willing to be still together.

And in that silence, Naomi heard everything she needed to hear. She heard the rhythm of his breathing. She heard the way his hands moved when he was thinking. She heard the letters he had never written. The words he had never said. The love he had never spoken.

"Hiroshi," she said.

"Yes?"

"I think I would like to write you a letter."

He smiled. "I would like to receive it."

"But I won't send it."

"Then I will come find it."

She laughed. He laughed. And the silence between them held them both.

They married in Kyoto, in the garden of Hiroshi's grandmother's house, under a cherry tree. Maren came from Paris. Naomi's mother came from San Francisco.

Hiroshi took photographs of the wedding — not the ceremony, not the guests, but the spaces between: the pause before the kiss, the silence after the vows. He titled the collection "Letters I Never Sent."

They were love letters. Every one. Not written in words, but in light. Not sent by mail, but by lens. Not read by eyes, but by hearts.
